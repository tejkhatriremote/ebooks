Adjusting Quality Settings

We covered audio quality briefly earlier in the chapter. Digital audio has three adjustable attributes that dictate how good it will sound: sample rate (8KHz to 44.1KHz), bit depth (8 or 16 bits), and mono/stereo setting. The easy way to think of audio quality settings is that more is generally better. The best is what Windows 2000 called CD Quality, which is 44.1KHz, 16-bit stereo—the same settings that your audio CD player uses for playing CDs. Of course, quality comes at a price. High sample rates and bit depths mean that more hard disk space is required to store the same amount of sound.

NT had three default quality settings: Telephone Quality, Radio Quality, and CD Quality. Telephone Quality is the lowest quality setting, but requires the least amount of disk space to store a recorded .wav file. Radio and CD Quality get progressively better, but as a result need more disk space. Generally speaking, unless you need really clean sound, the Radio Quality setting suffices for most applications and is a good trade-off between sound quality and file size. This is where audio codecs to compress a .wav file can come in very handy.

Windows 2000 actually includes these audio settings, and many more. There are four basic sample rates that can be used for sound files, 8KHz, 11.025KHz, 22.05KHz, and 44.1KHz. The higher the sample rate you choose the better the sound quality and the larger the file. Each format you choose (read that as each Codec you choose) will have its own file sizes for each of the sample rates and they will be displayed in the drop-down lists in the Sound Selection dialog box when you are setting preferences to record or converting an existing file.

For instance, Windows 2000’s Radio Quality setting is 22.050KHz, 8-bit mono, and a 10-second file at this setting would be about 215K. A 10-second .wav file with a setting of 44.1KHz, 16-bit stereo would be about 1.7MB—quite a difference. But using those same higher settings with the ADPCM codec, a 10-second .wav file would require only about 430K. Admittedly, the file with the higher settings is still eating up more disk space, but by using the ADPCM codec, you can get better quality for less disk real estate.

Let’s see how Sound Recorder’s audio quality settings work:

1.  Click the File pull-down menu and select New. Sound Recorder warns you that you haven’t saved changes to the file currently open (our test1.wav file). If you want to save your edits, go ahead and do so; otherwise, click No to continue.
2.  To change the quality setting on this new file, pull down the File menu, select Properties, and select Convert Now. In the Sound Selection dialog box, shown below, you can set the new .wav file’s quality settings. You can choose one of Windows 2000’s predefined quality settings or customize a specific quality setting of your own.

3.  For now, select Cancel, choose the Radio Quality setting in the Properties dialog box, and click OK.

Recording a Signal

To record an incoming signal, you need to connect either a microphone or a line-level device to your sound card’s Mic In or Line In connector. These should be marked on your sound card’s back panel.

Generally, PC sound card inputs don’t handle stronger signals well, so it’s better to adjust the device to output a signal at about 50 percent capacity. You can also adjust your recording levels using the Volume Control applet (discussed in the next section).

To record an incoming signal, click the Record button and wait several seconds until the Stop button is no longer grayed and has turned black. This indicates that Sound Recorder is recording. Don’t worry, you can delete the “leader” at the front of the .wav file later.

Running Volume Control

The Volume Control applet lets you set both playback and recording levels for all your sound card’s devices (WAV, MIDI, Audio CD, Microphone, and Line In). Launch the Volume Control applet by choosing Start Programs Accessories Entertainment and clicking its icon. Figure 7.15 shows the Volume Control window.


Figure 7.15  The Volume Control window

TIP:  Another, quicker way to launch the Volume Control applet is to go to the Taskbar and double-click the speaker icon you see on the right side. If you right-click it, you can also bring up the Audio Properties dialog box, which enables you to adjust your audio settings.

The first things you’ll notice are two types of slide controllers that set the levels—one set moves vertically and the other horizontally. Volume Control shows you playback levels first by default. Moving each slider up increases the level, and moving each down decreases the level. The sliders that move from left to right control the panning (balance) of each device’s output.
