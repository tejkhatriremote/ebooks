Mouse Drivers

Changing mouse drivers is easy in Windows 9x. In Windows 2000, the task’s pretty much the same. Let’s say that instead of the usual Microsoft serial mouse driver, you want to install a Logitech mouse. Follow these steps:

1.  Shut down your computer and install the new mouse. Restart your system.

NOTE:  Windows 2000 should automatically recognize your mouse without rebooting. If Windows 2000 doesn’t recognize your new mouse immediately, shut down and restart the computer.
2.  If the mouse wasn’t detected correctly during startup, you may have to open Control Panel and use the Add/Remove Hardware Wizard to detect it.
3.  You may be prompted to provide drivers for the mouse if Windows 2000 does not support it. Click the Have Disk button if you need to select the driver from a manufacturer’s disk.
4.  Specify the path where the driver files are located and click OK. Your new mouse driver is now successfully installed.

Click the Close button and restart your computer in order for the settings to take effect.

Keyboard Drivers

Changing keyboard drivers is similar to changing mouse drivers, and can be accomplished in the same way. Another method is to use Device Manager to change the drivers. To change a keyboard driver, you need to do the following:

1.  Shut down your computer and install the new keyboard. Restart your system.
2.  Open Device Manager by right-clicking My Computer, selecting Properties, then clicking the Hardware tab. Click the button for Device Manager to open it as shown in Figure 27.13.


Figure 27.13  Device Manager can be used to change drivers for your installed hardware.

3.  Open the branch for the keyboard and double-click the specific entry for the keyboard to open its Properties.
4.  Click the Driver tab, and click the Update Driver button. This will start the Upgrade Device Driver Wizard shown in Figure 27.14. Click Next.


Figure 27.14  The Upgrade Device Driver Wizard helps you to install a new driver.

5.  The wizard will prompt you to either search for proper drivers, or to specify the new driver. Click on the second option, and click Next.
6.  Now you will be prompted to choose the manufacturer and model of the new keyboard. Click the radio button to Show All Hardware of This Device Class to display a list of all of the keyboard drivers. Or, if you have drivers on disk, you can click the Have Disk button.

Click the Close button and restart the computer in order for the settings to take effect. And don’t forget to plug in your new keyboard.

Sound Cards

If you have a multimedia system or are thinking about upgrading one, you may want to install a sound card to enhance your presentations or games. Adding a sound card in Windows NT 4 meant several tricky steps. But in Windows 2000 it’s as easy as running the Add/Remove Hardware Wizard to detect the card and its settings.

Depending on the type of sound card you’re installing, you may have to make some settings on the card itself prior to adding it to Windows 2000. If the card is fully Plug and Play, then Windows 2000 will be able to detect and configure the card if there are enough available resources in your computer.

TIP:  Make sure that the addresses you specify aren’t already being used by another device, or you’ll have problems with both devices. You might want to keep a written record of the settings for all your devices. That way, if something does happen, the first thing you can do is look at the list to make sure that the new device doesn’t conflict with any existing ones.

Table 27.1 has a list of some of the most common settings for a sound card. You can use winmsd.exe to help you determine the available resources in your Windows 2000 computer prior to installing the sound card. Doing this before installing a new piece of hardware can save you a lot of grief if there aren’t any free resources. Figure 27.15 shows an example of the resources used by a sound card.
