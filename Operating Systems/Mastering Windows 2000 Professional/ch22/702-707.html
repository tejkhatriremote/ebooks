Types of Connectors

You can’t just go out and get cables and expect to run your network. First, you need some way of hitching your computer to the network via those cables. In other words, you now need to investigate connector possibilities.

The kind of connectors you need depends a lot on the kind of cable that you use. If you buy sale-priced Ethernet T-connectors in preparation for your future network, you’d better be prepared to go with an Ethernet network because Ethernet connectors won’t work with twisted-pair or fiber. Each kind of cable uses different connectors.

BNC Connectors

You’ll use three kinds of connectors on an Ethernet bus network:

T-connectors  Link the cables to the cards (see Figure 22.8). The T-connector plugs into the card you’ve installed in your PC. The cables connect to the crossbar of the T, as shown in Figure 22.9.

Terminators  Terminate the LAN on either end (see Figure 22.11).

Terminators are made for either 50-ohm or 75-ohm cable; 50-ohm terminators are usually the standard. Pay attention when you’re buying terminators because they won’t work if you mix them up. Most coax network cable systems need 50-ohm terminators.

RJ-45

Most unshielded twisted-pair cable terminates using connectors that look like chunkier versions of the connectors used to plug your telephone into the wall, as shown in Figure 22.12. These are called RJ-45, whereas your telephone uses RJ-11 connectors. One end plugs into your computer card, and the other plugs into a punch-down block in the wiring closet or a hub. We discuss network topologies in Chapter 23, but for the moment just understand that the way UTP terminates renders it unsuitable for any kind of wiring other than the star topology.


Figure 22.12  RJ-45 connector

D-Shells and Data Connectors

STP is bulkier than UTP and, correspondingly, needs a different connector. In a Token Ring topology, a D-shell connector links STP cable to the machine’s network card (Figure 22.13), whereas an IBM Token Ring Connector attaches the other end of the cable to the multistation access unit (MAU) or hub (Figure 22.14).

Fiber-Optic Connectors

Fiber-optic connectors are an important part of the cabling process. Unlike twisted-pair, in which the medium itself is the biggest source of signal loss, fiber-optic is most susceptible to signal dispersion at its connectors. Fiber-optic cable uses two kinds of connectors: screw-mounted adapters (SMA) and spring-loaded twist (ST). The ST connector uses a spring-loaded twist to clamp to the cable, whereas the SMA screws onto the end. ST connectors are more common than SMAs, and you can see one in Figure 22.15.

Wiring Systems

To help you get started, a number of companies have developed their own complete wiring systems. Here, we’ll discuss IBM’s and Ethernet’s wiring plans.

IBM Cabling System

IBM has developed standards for a wide variety of cables, all of which are either fiber-optic or, more commonly, twisted-pair. IBM uses eight types of cable in its wiring plan. Unlike the EIA/TIA (The Electronics Industry Association/Telecommunications Industry Association, remember?) categories of UTP, IBM does not rate its cables in order of immunity to RF noise. (Yes, we’ve arrived in acronym hell (AH). Get used to it; all LAN terminology looks like this.)

Please note: The following list is provided for your reference and does not have to be memorized. You don’t have to know everything; you just have to know where to find it.

Type 1  Shielded cable with two twisted-pairs made from solid (as opposed to stranded) wire. It’s used for data transmission, generally over Token Ring networks.
Type 2  Four unshielded pairs of solid wire for voice telephone communication and two more shielded data pairs in the same sheath. It’s designed so that you can run your telephone and your Token Ring network from the same cable.
Type 3  Four unshielded, solid twisted-pairs of wire for voice or data. This is IBM’s version of modern twisted-pair telephone wire.
Type 5  Two fiber-optic strands.
Type 6  Shielded cable with two twisted-pairs made from stranded wires. This is a lot like Type 1 but more flexible because it’s made from stranded (rather than solid) wire. It’s designed for data transmission, usually between a computer and a data jack in the wall.
Type 8  A special “under the carpet” shielded twisted-pair cable—it’s designed to make a smaller lump when it’s run under carpet.
Type 9  Plenum cable. Two shielded, twisted-pairs covered with a special flame-retardant coating, meant to be used between floors in a building. It’s extra fireproof to help prevent fire on one floor from following the cable to the rest of the building.
Type Z Fiber-optic cable. This is the new fiber-optic standard and it allows for distances of up to 130 nanometers (or microns).

Plenum cable, by the way, is a cable covered with plenum insulation. This particular kind of insulation is fire resistant, so it’s safer to use than other materials and no conduit is required for its installation in the office. Without plenum insulation, the cable itself could become the catalyst needed for a fire to spread very quickly.

WARNING:  Some fire ordinances require organizations to use only plenum-rated cable in certain situations—so before selecting cable, check your local fire codes to make sure the cable meets all fire codes in force.

To review, this is IBM’s proprietary wiring system, based on the logical ring and physical star topologies. Its standard rate of transmission is 4Mbps, but in 1989 IBM introduced a new version that transmits at 16Mbps. Token Ring generally uses UTP but can use fiber-optic cable if the network calls for it. One of the Token Ring architectures is IBM’s Systems Application Architecture (SAA), which supports peer-to-peer computing on a mainframe network.
