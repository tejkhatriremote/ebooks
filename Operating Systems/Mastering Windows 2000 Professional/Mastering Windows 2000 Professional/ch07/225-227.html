Audio and Video Codecs

The next two items in the Devices list on the Hardware tab are Video Compression Codecs and Audio Compression Codecs. A codec—short for coder-decoder—is a compression/decompression algorithm for recording or playing back audio and video data. Because multimedia files, especially .avi files, can be tremendously large, good compression is necessary for your PC to be able to handle all the data found in these files.

A key concept here is that there’s no such thing as “free” compression in multimedia. When a codec compresses audio or video, it tries to store as much information about the original data as possible, but winds up throwing some information out. The result is that when you play back a file that has been compressed with a codec, some information is missing, and the sometimes-noticeable degradation in sound or image quality is called artifacting. Some codecs are better at retaining information than others. A codec that produces a lot of artifacting is said to be lossy, because of the amount of original data missing from the playback.

Windows 2000 Video Codecs  Windows 2000 ships with five video codecs installed: Cinepak; Indeo and Indeo version 5.1; and two Microsoft codecs, Run Length Encoding (RLE) and Video 1. The Cinepak and Indeo are the most frequently used video codecs in multimedia programs, and they both deliver good image quality. The more lossy RLE and Video 1 codecs are holdovers from the Windows 3.1/Windows NT 3.5 days, and they were left in for the few Neanderthal applications still out there that use them.

A few other video codecs are used in multimedia titles, but the main one is MPEG. MPEG delivers superior image quality but is a more processor-intensive codec than Indeo or Cinepak. MPEG has required dedicated accelerator hardware in the past, but fast Pentium class chips and above can now play MPEG-encoded files with fairly good overall image quality, and you’ll be seeing a lot more MPEG in multimedia programs. You’ll also be seeing MPEG encoding used in DVD movies, which you may be playing on your computer under Windows 2000.

Windows 2000 Audio Codecs  Windows 2000 ships with nine audio codecs: TrueSpeech, IMA ADPCM, Microsoft ADPCM, Microsoft CCITT G.711, Microsoft GSM, Lernout and Hauspie, Microsoft G.723, Indeo audio, and Microsoft PCM converter. Two of these codecs, GSM and TrueSpeech, are for compressing recordings of speech. Because speech contains less audio “information” than music, speech codecs can often achieve good compression ratios (between 10:1 and 20:1) while maintaining acceptable sound quality, often similar to what a voice sounds like through a telephone.

Audio fidelity is much more important in music, so there are two flavors of a codec called Adaptive Differential Pulse Code Modulation (ADPCM)—one by the International Multimedia Association (IMA) and another by Microsoft—that can achieve a 4:1 compression ratio while sacrificing only a small amount of audio quality. For example, say you have a .wav file that’s 400K uncompressed. If you resave it with a wave editor and compress it with the ADPCM codec, the same file with the same amount of audio will now be only 100K, with only a slight degradation of audio quality.

Multimedia File Information

A cool new feature in Windows 2000 provides a quick way to get lots of information about multimedia files. Right-click any multimedia file and select Properties from the menu that appears to see a properties sheet for that file. You will see specific information about each of the media types. This information ranges from total playing time to sample rates and the codec the file uses for playback. Let’s take a look at three media types: a MIDI sequence, a .wav file, and an .avi file.
