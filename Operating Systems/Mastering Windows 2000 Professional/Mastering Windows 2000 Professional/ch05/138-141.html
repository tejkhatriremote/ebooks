Adjusting the Driver Settings

The last tab of the Display applet, the Settings tab, tweaks the video driver responsible for your video card’s capability to display Windows. These settings are more substantial than those that adjust whether dialog boxes are mauve or chartreuse because they load a different driver or bump your video card up or down into a completely different resolution and color depth. This changes the amount of information you can see on the screen at once (see Figure 5.21).


Figure 5.21  The Settings tab of the Display applet controls the video card’s device driver. With most video systems, the slider lets you adjust the screen resolution on the fly. Changing color depth may require a restart, however.

Next, let’s take a look at the individual settings on the Settings tab.

Colors

Let’s start with the color palette. Assuming your video card was properly identified when you installed Windows 2000, this drop-down list box includes all the legitimate options available for your card. As you may know, different video cards are capable of displaying differing numbers of colors simultaneously. Your monitor is not the limiting factor (with the exception of color LCD screens such as those on laptops, which do have limitations); the limitations have to do with how much RAM is on your video card. All modern analog color monitors for PCs are capable of displaying 16 million colors, which is dubbed True Color.

It’s possible that the drop-down list will include color amounts (called depths) that exceed your video card’s capabilities, in which case such a choice won’t have any effect. On the other hand, if your setting is 16 colors and your screen can support 256 or higher, Windows 2000 will look a lot prettier if you choose 256 and then choose one of the 256-color schemes from the Appearance tab.

TIP:  There is a trade-off between higher color depth and system resources: The more colors you choose to have available at any one time, the slower your video system performs. The price may be only marginal on systems with fast video cards and with today’s fast video cards, you probably won’t notice any slowdown, but keep it in mind.

Windows 2000 doesn’t require you to reboot after adding a new display driver the way NT did. Now you can choose the new settings and press either Apply or OK to make the change. Windows 2000 will tell you that it will apply the change for 15 seconds, at the end of which time it will revert back if you don’t decide to keep the new setting. If you click OK to this warning and you are able to see the new setting correctly, click OK to keep the new setting. This capability has replaced the need to use the Test button that was so prominent in earlier versions of NT.

Screen Area

The Screen Area setting is something avid Windows users have been wanting for years. With Windows 3.x, changing this parameter (essentially the screen resolution) meant running Windows Setup, choosing a different video driver, and rebooting the machine and Windows. Now, with the right video card, you can change the resolution as you work. Some jobs—such as working with large spreadsheets, databases, Computer Aided Design (CAD), or typesetting—are much more efficient with more data displayed on the screen. Because higher resolutions require a trade-off in clarity and make on-screen objects smaller, eyestrain can be minimized by going to a lower resolution, such as 640 × 480 pixels (a pixel equals one dot on the screen). Note that a relationship exists between the color depth and the resolution that’s available. This is because your video card can have only so much RAM on it. That RAM can be used to display extra colors or extra resolution, but not both. So, if you bump up the colors, you won’t have as many resolution options. If the dialog box won’t let you choose the resolution you want, try dropping the color palette setting to a lower color depth.

To change the Desktop Area setting:

1.  Run Control Panel and run the Display applet.
2.  Choose the Settings tab.
3.  Grab the slider and move it right or left. Notice how the little screen in the box indicates the additional room you’re going to have on your real screen to do your work (and also how everything will get relatively smaller to make this happen, because your monitor doesn’t get any larger!). Figure 5.22 illustrates this setting.


Figure 5.22  Change your screen resolution by dragging the slider. Here the user has chosen 800 × 600 pixels.

NOTE:  As of this writing, many laptops still have screens that can display only one resolution, often 800 × 600. This has been changing with the advent of the newer Thin Film Technology (TFT) LCD screens. Laptops now have much the same capabilities as desktop computers.
4.  Click OK to apply the changes to your settings. Windows 2000 will apply the changes and ask you if you want to keep the new settings. If you can’t see the new settings, don’t panic. Windows 2000 will automatically resume the previous settings in 15 seconds if you don’t confirm the new configuration.

TIP:  Even if you change the Palette setting, you should be able to change the display settings without restarting Windows 2000.
