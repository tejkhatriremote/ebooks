Setting the Pointer Motion

You can make two useful adjustments to the way the mouse responds to the motion of your hand—speed and trails (see Figure 5.25).


Figure 5.25  You can adjust the speed at which the mouse pointer moves and whether the cursor snaps to the default button.

Pointer speed is the speed at which the mouse pointer moves relative to the movement of the mouse. Believe it or not, mouse motion is measured in Mickeys! (Somebody out there has a sense of humor.) A Mickey equals 1/100 of an inch of mouse movement. The tracking-speed setting lets you adjust the relationship of Mickeys to pixels. If you want to be exact in your cursor movement, you’ll want to slow the tracking speed, requiring more Mickeys per pixel. However, this requires more hand motion for the same corresponding cursor motion. If your desk is crammed and your coordination is good, then you can increase the speed (fewer Mickeys per pixel). If you use the mouse with MS-DOS programs that use their own mouse driver, you might want to adjust the Windows mouse speed to match that of your other programs so you won’t need to mentally adjust when you use such non-Windows programs.

Incidentally, if you think the mouse runs too slowly in your non-Windows applications, there may be a fix. Contact your mouse’s manufacturer. For example, if you’re using a Logitech mouse, a program called Click that is supplied with the Logitech mouse lets you easily control its tracking. See the Logitech manual for details.

If your mouse driver supports the option, Mouse trails creates a shadow of the mouse’s path whenever you move it. Some people find it annoying, but for those who have trouble finding the pointer on the screen, it’s a blessing. Mouse trails are particularly helpful when using Windows on passive-matrix or dual-scan laptop computers, where the pointer often disappears when you move it.

Here are the steps for changing these items:

1.  Drag the speed slider one way or another to increase or decrease the motion of the pointer relative to your hand (or thumb in the case of a trackball) motion. Nothing may happen until you click Apply. Adjust as necessary. Try aiming for an item on the screen and see how well you succeed. Having the motion too fast can result in straining your muscles and holding the mouse too tight. It’s ergonomically more sound to use a little slower setting that requires more hand motion.
2.  Click OK or Apply to make it all official.

The other setting you normally see on the Motion tab is Snap to Default. This setting causes the cursor to automatically move to the default or highlighted button whenever you open a dialog box.

Mouse Hardware Settings

The last tab in the Mouse Properties dialog box is the Hardware tab. This dialog lets you view and configure the hardware settings for your installed pointing device. The Hardware tab includes a Troubleshoot button that will start the Mouse Troubleshooter in Online Help.

When you click the Properties button on the Hardware tab, the Mouse Properties dialog box appears. This dialog box gives you direct access to the driver settings that control your pointing device. From this dialog box, you can update the driver to a newer version using the Driver tab, change resources on the Resource tab, and make advanced settings on the Advanced Settings tab. These advanced settings are specific to the driver for your pointing device (see Figure 5.26).


Figure 5.26  Some mouse drivers have additional options built in. The Properties sheet of the Mouse applet gives you access to them.

Summary

As you have seen, most of the adjustments you can make through the Control Panel are not necessities as much as they are niceties. You can change settings to match the way your equipment behaves, but you can also change settings to match the way you like to behave. Remember, after you have changed a setting with the Control Panel, your alterations are stored in the Windows configuration Registry, which is the topic of a later chapter (Chapter 21, “The Registry,” to be precise). Your settings are reloaded each time you run Windows 2000 and stay in effect until you change them again with the Control Panel.
