Autodial Tab

The second tab controls the Autodial behavior of Windows 2000. Autodial is the ability to automatically dial a DUN connection when you try to access a network resource that is not present on your LAN. That is, if you have a shortcut on your Desktop that connects you to a shared folder on a server at work, you can click it when away from the office and your DUN will automatically offer to dial a connection for you.

This behavior can be a great assistance, but it can also be very annoying when you do not want to be reminded all the time about the network. Thus, the developers of Windows 2000 took pity on us and gave us the ability to disable autodial by location or session. The Autodial tab is shown in Figure 26.13.


Figure 26.13  The Autodial tab lets you enable autodial by location.

The Enable Autodial by Location box will contain a list of all of your TAPI locations. To enable autodial for each location, simply check the box beside that location. The other settings are to prompt you before autodialing and to disable autodial for this session.

Callback Tab

The third and final tab of the Dial-Up Preferences dialog box is the Callback tab. Windows 2000 supports callback security, wherein a DUN server can be configured to call you back as part of its verification when you dial in to it. Or maybe your company is simply being nice to you and absorbing the long distance charges by having the server offer to call you back at company expense. Figure 26.14 shows the settings on the Callback tab.


Figure 26.14  The Callback tab lets your computer respond to callback offers from a DUN server.

The options on the Callback tab include:

No Callback
This is the normal behavior of DUN in the past. When you dial a connection, the server answers and authenticates your credentials, and then you’re into the system.
Ask Me during Dialing When the Server Offers
This is the default setting for Windows 2000. This means that if the server offers callback, you will be prompted to decide whether to accept or not.
Always Call Me Back at the Number(s) Below
This setting means that you will only connect through Callback security and it will only connect to one pre-defined number. This does provide a measure of security when someone else gets hold of your computer and tries to access your company’s network. They may be able to dial and connect, but the server will hang up and dial back at only the numbers that have been pre-defined for your account.

Testing the DUN Connection

After you have configured your DUN connection, just double-click the shortcut to your Phonebook entry for an immediate connection. The lights of your (external) modem will flash, and if you have enabled the modem speaker, you should hear that distinctive squelching noise that indicates Windows 2000 is negotiating a connection.

Using Universal Naming Conventions (UNCs), you should now be able to access any network resource for which you have permission.

After you have established a connection for the first time, any of the following actions will activate Dial-Up Networking:

•  You select a network resource that is not part of your network.
•  A UNC calls for a network resource (\\server\public_).
•  An application calls for a network resource.

The power of this feature is that after you connect to the remote location for the first time (during this session of Windows 2000), anytime you perform any of the above functions, Windows 2000 automatically reestablishes the DUN connection with the network.

Using Shortcuts to Popular Network Information

Say that from home you would like to connect to your home directory at work. Here are the steps you follow:

1.  Open the My Computer folder.
2.  Open the Dial-Up Networking folder.
3.  Select MMCO OFFICE.
4.  Provide a password.

At this point, the system starts to negotiate the connection and to authenticate you to the network. After validation, you connect a network drive to your home directory on the network and then you can access your files.

Because you have to perform this process every time you access your home directory, you can allow shortcuts to come to the rescue. To create a shortcut to your home directory, you still follow the preceding steps the first time. Windows 2000 automatically creates a shortcut to this location when you click and drag your network home directory folder to the Desktop.

Now, if you want to get to your home directory, you double-click its shortcut. Windows 2000 automatically calls the network, validates you to the network (assuming your Windows 2000 logon and network logon are identical), and takes you to your home directory. If you are already connected to the network, the shortcut is still a useful way to fly to your home directory.

NOTE:  If you need to dial up to many different networks, shortcuts are a great way to organize all your frequently visited sites. Shortcuts also get you to the information on those networks that you use most frequently.
